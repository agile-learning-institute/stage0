# **Post Guidelines**

# Design Chat Exercises
Each Post in this channel represents a single Design Chat exercise. When contributing to an exercise you will add your reply to that post. Remember to:
- Keep observations brief - sticky with a dull sharpie
- Practice "yes and" thinking, writing, and speaking
- Give all your ideas, include magical or absurd thinking
- We are Observing as individuals, no discussion or cross-talk during the Observe phase
- Quantity over quality, better 10 crazy ideas than 1 "Best" idea

## Stakeholders 
The purpose of a stakeholders exercise is to focus our attention on the humans involved in the system. We want to think about the different types of users that the system will have, but we all also want to consider staffing, who our partners might be, what type of vendor relationships we might have. Sometimes people will describe their role is being one of many hats, let's capture those hats. 

### Observe

## Empathy Map
The purpose of an Empathy Map exercise is to focus our attention on a particular individual and try to empathize with their current realities. - Describe a persona - Name, Job, Hobbies, Family just enough to put a face in their heads.

### Observe
When recording your observations we like to ask you to make as many observations as you can and classify them into four different categories. We want to understand what this person:
- **does** - What have you seen them do? What do you imagine they do?
- **says** - What have you heard them say?
- **thinks** - How do they reason about their work
- **feels** - What emotions do they show/share?

For example.
```
does: Edits videos, Plays Disc Golf, drinks beer
says: Why are you doing that?
says: Let's go to Packs Pub
thinks: I'm the greatest editor in the world!
feels: Frustrated production takes so long
```
When you have emptied your mind of all observations give this post a thumbs up

### Reflect
Prompt: Review the empathy-map notes from Observe and replace them with a summary: use **level-1** headings `# Does`, `# Says`, `# Thinks`, and `# Feels`. Under each, use **level-2** and **level-3** headings for themes and for **causal threads** (reuse the same thread name across sections where it applies: behavior → what they say → how they reason → how they feel).

## Big ideas 
The purpose of a big ideas exercise is to capture big ideas about how to improve the experience of a user. We want to focus on the meaningful outcome of an experience and not get bogged down in details at this point. The exercise is more interested in the quantity of observations than quality. We'd rather have your 10 weirdest ideas than your one best idea.

### Observe
Give as many ideas as you can think of! Include absurd or magical thinking! Follow along, be inspired, and when recording your observations attempt to follow the format "<who> does <what> and then <wow>"" as in 
```
James clicks on the buy-it-now button, and then he can bypass the checkout process!
```

## Retrospective
The purpose of a retrospective exercise is to celebrate our successes and identify opportunities for continuous improvement. 

### Observe
Make as many observations as you can, grouped into four categories. Any **questions** you might have regarding the scope being closed out today, things that you thought **worked** well, things that need to **change**, and new **ideas** to try next time. For example:
```
question: When will we do this again?
worked: Jill rocked as team lead
change: All of our deliverables were late
ideas: T-Shirt size tasks before the next sprint
```

### Reflect
Collect the ideas from **Observe**, and show them as a markdown outline with the four category headings **Questions**, **Worked**, **Needs to change**, and **New ideas to try**; **bold** any line that restates the same theme as another (duplicate meaning). During reflection the team should answer questions, celebrate successes, acknowledge difficulties, and make sure that everything that needs to **change** has a new **idea** to try next time.

### Questions
- What do you all think of Design Chat Thinking? (Mike)
- Can we use AI for certain daily assignments that are given with a time limit? (Mary)

### Worked
- **Voice and chat modalities:** Mike called out chat and voice typing; Mary installed voice-to-text and learned how to use it.
- **Collaborative ideation:** Luther jumped into a think tank of ideas with the team; Daniel highlighted teamwork in generating ideas—“no bad idea”—and new concepts.

### Needs to change
- **Clarity of context, tasks, and wording:** Mike needed clearer distinction between the MentorHub Team and a MentorHub Mentee; Mary wanted a clearer picture of tasks and assignments; Luther tightened wording to clear up misunderstandings.
- **Balance single mentorship vs. team exercises:** Daniel felt the applications work well overall; still, focus more on single mentorship while keeping teamwork lessons applicable.

### New ideas to try
- Improve workshop and exercise scope definitions in the exercise descriptions (Mike).
- Think outside the box with educational incentives (Luther).
- Continue with examples for upcoming and current assignments (Mary).
- Find a streamlined top-down order for starting design applications (Daniel).
- End-of-week celebration—dinner, pizza, movie—to mark a good week together (Daniel).
- Give mentees special recognition with a trophy board or wall of accomplishments (Luther).

**Change → idea coverage:** scope/role confusion and unclear tasks pair with **clearer exercise scope definitions** and **ongoing assignment examples**; the AI-for-time-limited-assignments **question** can be answered alongside experiments on **educational incentives** or tooling; the single-mentorship focus pairs with how workshops are scoped next time.

### Make
Make assignments to answer unanswered questions, and to implement new ideas.
